The Meditation 'Hangover': Why You Feel More Anxious When You First Start Practicing

When I first committed to a daily meditation practice, I expected instant calm. I imagined myself sitting on a cushion, breathing deeply, and feeling my stress evaporate like mist in the sun. Instead, after my third day of trying to sit for fifteen minutes, I felt worse than when I started. I felt jumpy, irritable, and acutely aware of every single intrusive thought I had been successfully ignoring for years.

I called this my meditation “hangover.” It felt like I had gone to the gym for my mind and pulled a muscle I didn’t know I had. If you have started practicing mindfulness and suddenly feel like your anxiety has spiked, you aren’t doing it wrong. In fact, you are likely doing it exactly right.

The “Noise” Was Always There

Think of your mind like a room that hasn’t been cleaned in a decade. When you are constantly distracted by emails, podcasts, television, and social media, you are effectively keeping the lights off in that room. You can walk around the furniture, but you don’t really see the dust, the clutter, or the mess in the corners.

Meditation is the act of turning the lights on.

When you sit down to focus on your breath, you are choosing to observe your internal environment without the background noise of external stimulation. For the first time, you are actually hearing the thoughts you usually drown out. You aren’t creating new anxiety; you are finally noticing the anxiety that was already running in the background of your life. It feels like a spike in tension because you have removed the layers of distraction that were acting as a buffer.

The Physiological Shift

There is also a physical component to this initial discomfort. Many of us spend our days in a state of low-level “fight or flight.” Our shoulders are hiked toward our ears, our jaw is clenched, and our breathing is shallow. We are so accustomed to this tension that it feels like our “normal” state.

When you begin to practice mindfulness, you are intentionally signaling to your nervous system that it is time to downshift. When the body starts to relax, the sudden contrast can feel jarring. It is similar to how your legs might feel shaky after standing up from a long, cramped car ride. Your nervous system is recalibrating, and that transition phase can produce physical sensations like:

  • A sudden feeling of restlessness or an urge to “do something.”
  • Tingling or heat in the limbs as your posture shifts.
  • A temporary increase in heart rate as you become hyper-aware of your bodily functions.
  • Intrusive thoughts that seem louder because you are trying to ignore them (the “don’t think of a pink elephant” effect).

How to Handle the “Hangover”

If you are currently feeling this way, you don’t need to quit. You just need to change your approach. Most beginners try to force themselves into a state of “nothingness,” which is a recipe for frustration. Instead, try these three adjustments:

1. Lower the time threshold If you are trying to sit for 20 minutes, stop immediately. Start with three to five minutes. Your goal right now isn’t “enlightenment”; it is simply building the habit of being present. If five minutes feels like too much, go down to two. It is better to do two minutes of consistent practice than to do twenty minutes once a week and feel like a failure.

2. Stop fighting the thoughts The biggest mistake beginners make is judging themselves for having thoughts. You might think, “I’m supposed to be meditating, but I’m just worrying about my taxes, so I’m bad at this.” That judgment is actually a new layer of stress. Try to treat your thoughts like passing cars on a street. You can see the car (the thought), you can acknowledge it is there, but you don’t have to chase it down the street.

3. Use a physical anchor If your mind is racing, focus on a specific, high-sensory physical sensation. Instead of just “breathing,” count the feeling of your feet pressing into the floor. Feel the texture of your pants against your skin. By grounding your attention in a physical sensation, you give your brain a specific task, which makes it harder for it to wander into the anxiety-provoking “what ifs.”

Moving Through the Discomfort

The “hangover” period is temporary. It is a sign that you have moved past the surface level of your stress and are beginning to engage with the deeper patterns of your mind. It is uncomfortable, but it is not dangerous. It is the friction of change.

If you find that your anxiety remains consistently high or starts to impact your daily function, it is worth acknowledging that mindfulness is a tool for self-awareness, not a replacement for professional clinical support. However, for most people starting out, this feeling is simply the “cleaning” process. Once you get past the initial dust storm, you will find that the room becomes much clearer, and the quiet you were looking for begins to settle in naturally.

As you look to refine your practice and move toward a more consistent, peaceful experience, consider your next steps carefully. You may want to look into guided programs or specific techniques that help you bridge the gap between initial frustration and long-term stability.
