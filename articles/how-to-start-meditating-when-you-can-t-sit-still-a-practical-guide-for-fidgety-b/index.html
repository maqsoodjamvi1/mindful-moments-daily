If you have ever tried to meditate by sitting cross-legged on a floor cushion, only to find yourself obsessing over an itchy ankle or a half-finished email by the thirty-second mark, you aren’t doing it wrong. You just have a high-energy nervous system.

For years, I assumed meditation was a task for people who were naturally calm. I thought it required a blank mind and a body that could remain frozen for twenty minutes. Every time I tried to force myself into that stillness, I ended up more frustrated than when I started. It wasn’t until I stopped trying to mimic the “Zen master” aesthetic and started working with my natural restlessness that the practice actually stuck.

Stop Trying to “Empty” Your Mind

The biggest myth about meditation is that you need to stop thinking. If you are a fidgety person, your brain is likely wired to be a problem-solving machine. When you sit down and try to force that machine to shut off, it panics and produces more noise to compensate.

Instead of trying to reach a state of void, treat your mind like a browser with too many tabs open. You aren’t trying to close the browser; you are just trying to stop clicking on every link that pops up. When you notice yourself thinking about your grocery list or that awkward thing you said at lunch, don’t judge it. Just acknowledge, “Oh, that’s a thought,” and let it drift. Think of it like watching cars pass on a highway from a bridge. You see them, but you don’t have to jump into traffic.

Use Movement as Your Anchor

If your body refuses to be still, don’t fight it. Use your movement as the meditation itself. This is often called “active mindfulness.”

One of my favorite ways to do this is through “walking meditation.” You don’t need a forest or a beach. You can do this in your living room or a quiet hallway. Here is how:

  • Pick a pace: Walk slightly slower than you normally would.
  • Focus on the sensation: Feel your heel hit the floor, then the arch, then the ball of your foot. Notice the shift in weight in your hips.
  • Sync your breath: Try to take two steps while inhaling and two steps while exhaling.
  • Reset: Every time your brain wanders to your to-do list, bring your attention back to the physical sensation of your feet touching the ground.

By giving your legs a job, you satisfy the physical need for stimulation, which makes it much easier for your brain to settle into the present moment.

Try “Tactile” Grounding

Sometimes, the best way to quiet a restless mind is to give your hands something to do. I often keep a smooth stone or a piece of heavy fabric on my desk. If I’m having a high-anxiety day and need a “reset,” I hold that object and focus entirely on its temperature, weight, and texture.

This is a form of meditation. By forcing your brain to process sensory data—is the stone cold? Is it smooth or jagged?—you pull your focus away from the loop of anxious thoughts in your head. You are meditating on the object.

Short Bursts Over Long Sessions

When you are just starting, do not aim for twenty minutes. If you are fidgety, twenty minutes feels like an eternity. Start with three minutes.

Set a timer on your phone so you don’t have to keep checking the clock. If you can manage three minutes of sitting, standing, or walking while staying connected to your breath, you have succeeded. Consistency beats duration every time. It is better to meditate for three minutes every day for a week than to struggle through one twenty-minute session that leaves you feeling like a failure.

Create a “Landing Strip”

Transitioning from a busy day into a meditation session is hard. If you jump straight from a high-stress meeting to your meditation cushion, your heart rate is still elevated.

Create a buffer zone. Before you start, do something physical for two minutes:

  • Do ten jumping jacks to burn off excess adrenaline.
  • Drink a full glass of cold water.
  • Stretch your neck and shoulders to release the “computer hunch.”

By physicalizing the end of your workday, you signal to your body that it is safe to shift gears.

Don’t Wait for the “Perfect” Feeling

You might expect meditation to feel like a warm, fuzzy wave of relaxation. Sometimes it does, but often it feels like nothing at all. You might sit for five minutes, feel bored, feel annoyed at the neighbor’s dog barking, and then your timer goes off.

That wasn’t a bad session. That was a successful session. The point of meditation is to build the “muscle” of noticing when your attention has wandered and bringing it back. Every time you catch yourself fidgeting or thinking, and you gently guide your focus back to your breath or your body, you are doing a “rep.” The more you catch yourself, the stronger your focus becomes.

As you start to incorporate these small, manageable shifts into your daily routine, you will likely find that the restlessness becomes a little less loud. The next step is to find a structure that fits your specific schedule and temperament, ensuring this practice becomes a permanent part of your toolkit for managing daily life.
